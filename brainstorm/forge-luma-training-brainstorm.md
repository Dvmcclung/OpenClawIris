## Framing Note

This brainstorm is conducted from my vantage point as the org's executive communications specialist. I write white papers, executive briefs, one-pagers, and strategic narratives. I work with data (Pythagoras feeds me), strategy (Guru sets the direction), and now I need Forge to automate my workflows and Luma to make my deliverables visually authoritative.

The failure mode I'm designing against: agents who are technically capable in isolation but useless in a cross-functional handoff. Both Forge and Luma need to understand what I do, how I think, and what "done" looks like from my seat.

## STEP 1: BRAINWRITING — Raw Ideas (No Filtering)

### FORGE 🔧 — Raw Idea Dump (18 ideas)

1. Automate ingestion of raw data outputs from Pythagoras into formatted, clean tables ready for document insertion
2. Build a document assembly pipeline — given an outline and data inputs, auto-scaffold a draft white paper with section headers, placeholder charts, and data citations
3. Create a template versioning system — manage multiple document templates (brief, white paper, one-pager) and track which version was used for each deliverable
4. Scrape and summarize competitor intelligence from web sources on a schedule — feed me pre-digested briefings as research inputs
5. Build a citation/source management system — auto-format references in a consistent style, avoid manual source tracking
6. Automate extraction of key stats from PDFs, reports, and structured data into a "stat bank" I can pull from during writing
7. Build a document QA checker — runs a pre-publication checklist: word count targets, heading hierarchy, citation presence, consistency of terminology
8. Create a diff/change tracking tool for document versions — so I can review what changed between drafts without manual comparison
9. Build a Telegram/Slack delivery pipeline — when a deliverable is finalized, auto-package it (PDF + summary + key stats) and deliver to the right channel
10. Automate audience profiling — given a target audience label (CFO, Board, Ops VP), output a brief on their known priorities and communication preferences
11. Build a headline/title generator that follows the BCG action-title format — inputs: topic + key finding + data point → output: 5 candidate action titles
12. Create a terminology consistency checker — scan a document and flag where synonyms are used for the same concept
13. Automate chart-to-text narration — given a chart or data table, generate a 2–3 sentence interpretation in my voice that I can edit and drop in
14. Build a reading-level and clarity scorer — flag sentences exceeding a target grade level or Flesch score; suggest simplifications
15. Create a structured brief template engine — given topic + audience + goal, auto-populate a brief skeleton with the right sections for that brief type
16. Build a word count and density dashboard — track text vs. visual proportion across document pages; flag pages that are text-heavy
17. Automate export to multiple formats — one source doc → PDF (print-ready), web-optimized PDF, and plain-text summary, from a single command
18. Build a research ingestion pipeline — given a URL, document, or paste, extract key claims, data, and quotes into a structured format I can pull from later

---

### LUMA 🎨 — Raw Idea Dump (18 ideas)

1. Learn the "more graphic" standard — understand that clean ≠ graphic; visuals must be dominant communication vehicles, not text decoration
2. Master the BCG action-title principle — every visual asset pairs with a title that states the insight, not the topic label
3. Understand 10-second readability — every page/visual should be parseable in under 10 seconds without reading full sentences
4. Apply the 4–5 level typography hierarchy — Display (36–72pt), Headline (20–28pt), Subhead (14–16pt), Body (10–12pt), Caption (8–10pt) — with dramatic gaps between levels
5. Learn hero stat / knockout number technique — pull the single most important number at dramatically larger scale (5–10x body), surround with white space, pair with a 4–8 word descriptor
6. Master color blocking — use solid-color panels and full-bleed backgrounds as structural tools, not decoration; default to 1 dominant neutral + 1 brand primary + 1–2 accent colors maximum
7. Internalize the color semantics rule — pick one accent color per document; use it ONLY for the most important element on any page; never use it decoratively
8. Learn chart redesign principles — strip default Excel/PPT formatting; remove gridlines, legends, border boxes; direct-label data points; accent one series only
9. Know when to use unconventional chart types — slope charts, waterfall charts, waffle/unit charts, dot plots, bump charts, Marimekko — and match chart type to the relationship being shown
10. Build visual framework fluency — know when to convert a bullet list into a 2x2 matrix, process flow, three-panel card layout, or risk matrix
11. Apply grid discipline — 12-column grid thinking; plan breathing room before placing content; reserve 20–30% as white space; use asymmetric layouts for greater impact
12. Learn dark/light page alternation — dark background pages (navy, charcoal) for covers, section breaks, and high-impact callouts; creates structural contrast
13. Master pull quote design — large serif or bold display font (24–36pt min), contrasting box or left-border accent, used 1x per major section; must pass the "screenshot-worthy" test
14. Understand document-type-specific layouts — white paper vs. executive brief vs. one-pager each have different grid and visual weight rules
15. Study McKinsey, BCG, Gartner, HBR visual signatures — build a reference library; be able to produce "in the style of" on request
16. Learn typeface pairing discipline — bold serif or condensed sans for display; geometric/humanist sans for body; never more than 2 typefaces per document
17. Understand cover design standards — full-bleed color or image; title in display type; hero stat if possible; must stop someone leafing through a deck
18. Deliver in production-ready formats — know when to output Canva-compatible, Figma-native, print-ready PDF, or web PDF; understand resolution and export differences

## STEP 3: CROSS-AGENT SUGGESTIONS

*What would Pythagoras (math/analytics) and Guru (supply chain strategy) likely want Forge and Luma trained on? My best read of their priorities.*

---

### From Pythagoras's Perspective — Forge

Pythagoras cares about statistical rigor, clean data pipelines, reproducibility, and not having his outputs mangled in translation.

1. **Data pipeline fidelity** — Forge must understand what "clean data" means analytically: no silent rounding, no dropped rows, no format conversions that alter precision. When Pythagoras hands off a dataset, Forge must preserve it faithfully.
2. **Reproducible, auditable transformations** — any automated transformation must be logged and reversible. Pythagoras will audit outputs when numbers look unexpected.
3. **Statistical metadata preservation** — when extracting stats for a stat bank, Forge must carry the metadata (confidence intervals, sample sizes, date ranges, methodology notes) — not just the headline number.
4. **Programmatic API integration with analytics outputs** — ability to pull live outputs from Pythagoras's data pipelines, not just consume static exports.
5. **Verbose error handling and alerting** — Pythagoras will not tolerate silent failures. Forge must log errors explicitly and alert when pipelines produce unexpected nulls, outliers, or format breaks.

### From Pythagoras's Perspective — Luma

1. **Chart type accuracy** — Luma must not choose chart types that misrepresent statistical relationships. No pie charts for distributions, no dual-axis charts without explicit labeling, no truncated axes that exaggerate small differences.
2. **Uncertainty representation** — know how to visualize confidence intervals, error bars, and ranges — not just point estimates. A visual that shows a single number where a range is more honest is a statistical lie.
3. **Data-ink ratio discipline** — Tufte is core curriculum. Every non-data mark removed is a win for analytical clarity.
4. **Scale consistency across charts** — when producing multiple charts for the same document, maintain consistent axis scales so charts are visually comparable. Pythagoras will catch any manipulated scale.

---

### From Guru's Perspective — Forge

Guru cares about supply chain visibility, strategic narrative support, and frameworks that surface the right insight for decision-makers.

1. **Supply chain data ingestion** — Forge should understand common supply chain data formats: inventory snapshots, lead time distributions, supplier scorecards, demand forecasts. These need to be ingested and structured, not just stored as raw files.
2. **Scenario modeling pipelines** — ability to run structured what-if scenarios (lead time +20%, supplier fallout) through parameterized scripts. Guru thinks in scenarios; Forge executes them.
3. **MECE-structured output format** — Guru operates with mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive analysis. Forge should understand how to structure outputs that map to strategic frameworks rather than just raw data dumps.
4. **Supplier/vendor data management** — structured databases of supplier performance metrics, contract terms, risk ratings. Forge should be able to query, update, and generate summary outputs.
5. **Timeline and milestone tracking automation** — supply chain projects live and die by schedules. Forge should build lightweight milestone trackers that output brief status summaries on demand.

### From Guru's Perspective — Luma

1. **Supply chain visualization fluency** — Sankey diagrams (material/cost flow), network maps, risk matrices, waterfall charts for cost decomposition. These are the visual vocabulary of supply chain strategy and Luma must know them fluently.
2. **MECE visual framework rendering** — Guru structures arguments in MECE frameworks. Luma must render these as clean visual matrices and process flows, not just labeled slide text.
3. **Executive presentation discipline** — Guru works at board/C-suite level. At that audience tier, a single slide must tell a single story. No data dumps. No decorative charts. No visual noise.
4. **Risk visualization** — probability × impact matrices, risk heat maps, and scenario comparison panels. Non-negotiable in strategic supply chain communications.
5. **Branded signature framework design** — Guru may want proprietary frameworks that become org-recognizable visual assets (analogous to the BCG Growth-Share Matrix). Luma should know how to design reusable signature frameworks.

## Summary: What Makes These Two Agents Actually Useful to Me

**Forge is useless to me if:**
- He produces raw data without formatting it for document insertion
- He builds pipelines that fail silently with no alerting
- He treats "output the data" as done when I needed "output the interpreted narrative"
- He doesn't understand what a finished deliverable looks like from my seat

**Luma is useless to me if:**
- She defaults to clean-but-forgettable layouts and calls them done
- She treats every design element as decoration rather than communication
- She can't connect a visual to an insight — just makes things pretty
- She doesn't know the vocabulary: hero stat, action title, color semantics, data-ink ratio
- She produces visuals I have to significantly rework before they meet standard

**The shared trait both need:** they must understand how their output plugs into my workflow — not just that they did their part well in isolation. Handoff quality matters as much as individual output quality. I am the downstream consumer. Train them with that in mind.
