## 1. Core Philosophy

Anett Grant's coaching is built on a single premise: **speaking (and by extension, leading communication) is not about information delivery — it is about connection, influence, and impact.**

### Foundational Principles

**Hard vs. Strong (Thought 1)**  
The single most fundamental distinction. Hard speakers talk *at* audiences (energy reflects off); Strong speakers talk *with* audiences (energy is absorbed). Hard = staccato, rigid, flat. Strong = resonant, fluid, multi-dimensional. The goal in all communication is strength, not hardness.

**The Internal Focus (Thought 3)**  
Powerful speakers maintain consciousness of three internal factors simultaneously: *what you're thinking* (your message), *what you're feeling* (your emotional state), and *what your body is doing* (physical expression). Losing awareness of any one of these degrades impact.

**Openness vs. Intimacy (Thought 7)**  
Openness = willingness to share ideas broadly. Intimacy = the personal, one-on-one quality that makes an audience feel you're speaking directly to them. The most powerful speakers achieve both simultaneously — they're open enough to share freely but intimate enough to connect personally.

**Appealing vs. Pleasing (Thought 42)**  
Appealing to your audience means giving them what they *need* to hear — truth, substance, challenge. Pleasing them means giving them what they *want* to hear. Leaders who only please lose credibility; leaders who appeal build authority.

**Influencing vs. Educating (Thought 34)**  
Educating fills minds with information. Influencing changes minds — and then behavior. Influencing requires building desire first, then providing information. Most business communication tries to educate when it should be influencing.

**Composure as Foundation (Thought 9)**  
Composure means managing the space between stimulus and response. It is not passivity — it is the disciplined ability to stay grounded under pressure. Without composure, no other communication skill can function properly.

**Strength vs. Aggression (Thought 12)**  
Strength persuades through substance and presence. Aggression tries to overpower. Aggression damages relationships and creates resistance; strength builds credibility and trust.

**Speaking Is Like Music; Writing Is Like Film (Thought 49)**  
Speaking relies on rhythm, tempo, dynamics, and live performance — like music. Writing relies on structure, framing, visual pacing, and considered construction — like film. This distinction is critical: what works in one medium often fails in the other.

**The Role of Images (Thoughts 10, 21, 22)**  
Images (vivid, specific word-pictures) are not decorative — they are the primary vehicle for comprehension, retention, and emotional connection. Facts without images fail to stick. The more difficult the message, the more essential the image.

**Stages of Presenters (Thought 78)**  
Speakers evolve through stages: (1) Self-conscious — focused on self; (2) Message-conscious — focused on content; (3) Audience-conscious — focused on audience response. Stage 3 is mastery. Most business communicators are stuck at Stage 1 or 2.

## 2. The Core-Satellite System

The Core-Satellite System is Anett Grant's primary structural framework for organizing any presentation or communication. The schematics documents define six components:

### The Structure
```
[INTRODUCTION]
       ↓
    [CORE]  ←  The central message/argument — one clear, compelling idea
   /  |  \
[SAT] [SAT] [SAT]  ←  Satellites: supporting points, each with categories
   \  |  /
    [CORE]  ←  Linkage back to the core message
       ↓
[SUMMARY & LINKAGE]
```

### Components

**The Introduction**  
Must establish linkage with the audience immediately. Not a joke or quote — an "ah-so" moment that connects your message to the audience's reality. The formula: establish relevance, then promise value.

**Developing the Core**  
The core is a single, focused central message — not a topic, but a position. It is the one thing you want your audience to walk away believing or doing. Everything else orbits it. Without a strong core, satellites fly off in all directions.

**The Satellites with Categories**  
Satellites are the major supporting arguments. Each satellite contains categories (sub-points). Effective satellites:
- Are parallel in structure
- Each independently support the core
- Are introduced with clear signal language
- Are developed with images, stories, and facts

**Rhythmic Builds (Thought 45)**  
A rhythmic build is a structured, repeating pattern that creates momentum and emotional intensity. Three or more parallel statements building toward a climax. Think: "We will grow. We will lead. We will win." The rhythm itself carries conviction.

**Developing the Questions**  
Questions are rhetorical tools that draw audiences in. Used to open sections, transition between satellites, or challenge the audience's existing assumptions. Questions signal "I'm aware you have doubts — let's address them."

**Summary and Linkage Back to the Core**  
The close is not a summary of facts — it is a return to the core message with renewed emphasis. It answers: "So what does all this mean, and what do I want you to do now?" Strong closes create momentum toward action.

### The ABC Approach (Thought 44)
A structured method for any communication:
- **A:** Answer the question / State the core position first
- **B:** Background / provide supporting evidence (satellites)  
- **C:** Call to action / Link back to core and specify next steps

## 4. Fast Company Article Summaries

### **FC 1: How to Command a Room Like Stephen Colbert**
Using the concept of gravitas, Grant outlines six concrete strategies for commanding attention: (1) communicate with singular focus — no mixed signals; (2) use selective eye contact to create personal connection across a room; (3) demonstrate awareness of space through purposeful positioning; (4) use silence as a power tool; (5) modulate pace and energy to signal confidence; (6) let your physical presence match the weight of your message. Key insight: a speaker's tone, appearance, and demeanor are nine times more salient than actual content.

### **FC 2: Answering Tough Questions**
Grant teaches a structured approach to hostile or difficult questions: acknowledge, bridge, respond. Don't react defensively — pause, reframe, and answer from your core position. The listener's confidence in you is determined more by *how* you answer than *what* you answer. Never let a challenging question destabilize your composure.

### **FC 3: Delivering Bad News**
Bad news should never be buried or softened to the point of obscurity. Grant's framework: be direct about the situation first, provide context for *why* it happened, then pivot quickly to what comes next. The goal is to demonstrate leadership — not to avoid discomfort. How you deliver bad news defines your credibility as a leader more than the news itself.

### **FC 4: Crafting a Rousing Toast**
A toast is a miniature Core-Satellite presentation: establish connection with the honoree, tell a specific story that illustrates their defining quality, and close with a forward-looking celebration. Specificity is everything — generic praise lands flat. The best toasts are personal, brief, and end on a rising note.

### **FC 5: How to Eliminate Filler Words**
Filler words (um, uh, like, you know) are symptoms of two things: processing lag and anxiety about silence. The fix is not to suppress the filler consciously but to make peace with pause. Silence communicates confidence; fillers communicate uncertainty. Grant recommends recording yourself, identifying your specific fillers, then practicing deliberate pausing in their place.

### **FC 6: Conquer Your Fear of Public Speaking**
Speaking anxiety is nearly universal. Grant reframes it: tension is not the enemy — it's the energy that gives great speaking its aliveness. The goal is to channel the energy outward toward the audience rather than inward toward self-monitoring. Practical techniques: preparation reduces fear; physical movement releases tension; focusing on the audience's needs (not your own performance) redirects attention productively.

### **FC 7: Master Small Talk**
Small talk is not trivial — it is the gateway to all business relationships. Grant's core insight: effective small talk is about genuine curiosity in the other person, not performance. Ask questions that invite stories, not yes/no answers. Listen actively and build on what's shared. Small talk is the first test of whether you can connect.

### **FC 8: Use Gestures When Speaking**
Gestures fall into two categories: image gestures (which show size, shape, movement, and reinforce the literal content of words) and habitual gestures (nervous tics that carry no meaning). Image gestures increase comprehension and retention. Grant teaches: gesture from the shoulder, not the wrist; make gestures that match the scale of the room; let gestures precede or accompany words, never follow them.

### **FC 9: Speaking from the Heart**
Speaking from the heart is not about emotion — it's about commitment. It means being fully present with your conviction about the idea you're communicating. Audiences sense immediately when a speaker doesn't believe what they're saying. The technical elements of speaking (structure, delivery) only work if there's genuine conviction underneath them.

### **FC 15: How to Respond to Hostile Questions**
Hostile questions are opportunities disguised as attacks. Grant's framework: maintain composure first (don't match the hostility), acknowledge the concern (not agreement with it), redirect to your position, and respond substantively. Never let a hostile questioner take control of the room's emotional temperature. The speaker who stays calm under attack earns credibility.

### **FC 16: Strong vs. Aggressive**
Strength in communication means making your case with conviction while respecting the listener's intelligence. Aggression means using force, volume, or dismissal to overpower pushback. Aggressive communicators win battles and lose relationships. Strong communicators build both agreement and respect. The line between them is often tone and the treatment of opposing views.

### **FC 17: How to Use Props**
Props create tangible, memorable anchors for abstract ideas. Rules for effective props: introduce the prop before displaying it; connect it explicitly to your core message; remove it once the point is made (lingering props steal focus). The best props are simple, visible from across a room, and intrinsically connected to the idea — not just decorative.

### **FC 18: Four Steps for Communicating Change**
Grant's framework for change communication: (1) Acknowledge the current reality — don't pretend things aren't changing; (2) Explain the *why* — connect the change to a clear rationale that serves the audience's interests; (3) Paint the future picture vividly — use images of where you're headed; (4) Define the specific role each person plays. Change communication fails when it skips steps 1 and 4.

### **FC 19: Communicating with Upper Management**
The cardinal rule of upward communication: lead with your conclusion, not your methodology. Senior leaders want judgment, not journey. Structure: state your recommendation first, provide supporting rationale, anticipate objections, close with a clear ask. Never make executives work to find your point. Demonstrate leadership by owning your position rather than presenting 'here are the options.'

### **FC 20: Speaking to Multicultural Audiences**
Cultural context profoundly affects how communication is received. Grant's five tips: (1) avoid idioms and colloquialisms that don't translate; (2) use universal images (numbers, shapes, physical actions); (3) speak at a moderate pace and with clear enunciation; (4) check comprehension explicitly; (5) honor different cultural norms around directness and hierarchy. The goal is communicating clearly, not performing cultural awareness.

### **FC 21: Fail-Proof Formula for Powerful Stories**
Grant's story formula: (1) Set the scene — specific place, time, character; (2) Create conflict — something had to be overcome; (3) Resolve the conflict — show what happened; (4) Connect to the message — make the relevance explicit. The most common story failure is starting with background (boring) instead of tension (engaging). Stories work because they activate the same brain regions as lived experience.

### **FC 22: How to Be Great on Video**
Video strips away most of the physical presence cues that make in-person speaking effective. Compensating strategies: look directly into the camera (not the screen); reduce body movement (camera amplifies fidgeting); increase vocal energy slightly to compensate for technical compression; use a clean background; ensure lighting is front-facing. On video, your voice and eyes carry the entire connection load.

### **FC 23: The Right Way to Praise Your Team**
Effective praise is specific, timely, and connected to impact — not generic ('great job'). Grant's framework: name the specific behavior or decision, explain its impact, connect it to the team's larger purpose. Generic praise lands flat because it signals the speaker didn't really observe. Specific praise builds genuine motivation because it proves you were paying attention.

### **FC 24: 5 Ways to Improve Speaking and Writing Simultaneously**
Speaking and writing are distinct cognitive acts (different brain regions) that share common principles. Grant's five bridges: (1) adjust to audience attention level (speaking must fight distractions; writing must hold reading attention); (2) use images in both (vivid specificity works in both media); (3) build in rhythm and variation; (4) match vocabulary to audience; (5) always have a clear core message. The biggest mistake: writing the way you speak (stream-of-consciousness) or speaking the way you write (formal, dense, sequential).

### **FC 25: What to Do When Someone Steals Your Idea**
When credit is taken for your work or ideas, the worst response is emotional accusation. Grant's approach: re-establish ownership calmly and publicly by re-presenting your idea in the next appropriate forum, naming your contribution matter-of-factly. Communicate with confidence rather than grievance. The goal is to rebuild the record without making it a conflict.

### **FC 26: Everyone Hated Your Presentation. Now What?**
A failed presentation is data, not identity. Grant's recovery protocol: (1) separate what you can control from what you can't; (2) seek specific feedback (not 'what went wrong' but 'what one thing would have most improved it'); (3) identify one thing to practice before the next presentation; (4) reframe the failure as a necessary step in development. Most speakers avoid analyzing failures — the ones who do improve fastest.

### **FC 27: 4 Musical Principles for Better Speaking**
Grant draws on music theory to illuminate speaking dynamics: (1) Tempo — vary your speaking speed; slow down for emphasis, speed up for energy; (2) Rhythm — create patterns that listeners can feel, then break them for impact; (3) Dynamics — vary volume deliberately, not randomly; (4) Rests — silence is a musical element; pauses create anticipation and emphasis. Monotone speakers violate all four principles simultaneously.

### **FC 28: How to Become a Better Speaker This Year**
Grant's practical improvement plan: (1) Record yourself regularly — you cannot improve what you cannot observe; (2) Focus on one skill at a time, not wholesale reinvention; (3) Seek deliberate feedback (specific, behavioral, actionable); (4) Practice in low-stakes environments before high-stakes ones; (5) Study great speakers analytically — what specifically are they doing? The most common barrier: people only practice content, never delivery.

### **FC 29: How to Speak Off the Cuff Like a Pro**
Impromptu speaking is not about winging it — it's about applying a rapid structural framework. Grant's PREP formula for spontaneous speaking: Point → Reason → Example → Point (restated). The structure gives you something to navigate even when there's no time to prepare. The goal is to sound conversational, not canned — so internalize the pattern, not a script.

### **FC 30: What Your Hand Gestures Are Saying**
Gestures operate below conscious awareness for most audiences — they convey confidence, uncertainty, authority, or nervousness before any words register. Specific signals: hands hidden (insecurity), hands at face (deception or anxiety), gestures above the waist (confidence), mirroring gestures (rapport). The most powerful gesture position is hands visible, relaxed, at navel level when not gesturing — the 'ready position.'

### **FC 31: Three Quick Tips for Presence and Power**
Three immediately actionable techniques: (1) Arrive before your audience and own the physical space; (2) Use silence before beginning — don't start speaking until you're fully present; (3) End with a strong close — the last thing audiences hear is what they remember. Most speakers rush the opening (nerves) and trail off the close (relief it's ending). Both undercut presence.

### **FC 32: How Candidates Look (or Fail to Look) Spontaneous**
Grant analyzes political debates to reveal what 'spontaneous' actually looks like in practiced communicators: specific language over generic; varied sentence structure; emotional authenticity without emotional volatility; using silence rather than filler. Over-preparation without flexibility produces robotic delivery; under-preparation produces incoherence. The sweet spot is 'prepared flexibility' — knowing your material cold enough that you can respond to anything.

### Audio 01: What Are the Differences Between Influencing and Educating?
Influencing changes behavior by first building desire; educating provides information without necessarily changing anything. Leaders who default to education often find their communication falls flat because they skip the step of creating motivation. The sequence matters: influence by creating a felt need first, then fill that need with information.

### Audio 02: What Are the Differences Between Presenting One-on-One and Presenting to Groups?
One-on-one presentation allows constant feedback loops and real-time adjustment. Group presentations require the speaker to do all the audience monitoring proactively — read the room, adjust energy, manage non-reactivity. The core message should be the same; the delivery style must flex significantly. Groups require more structure, more vocal projection, and more deliberate use of eye contact.

### Audio 03: What Are the Differences Between Hard and Strong?
The foundational distinction in Grant's system. Hard: staccato, rigid, flat, talks *at* audience, energy reflects off. Strong: resonant, fluid, multi-dimensional, talks *with* audience, energy is absorbed. Strength creates connection; hardness creates distance. Most executives default to hardness under pressure.

### Audio 04: What Are the Differences Between Sharing Your Experiences and Bragging?
Sharing experiences establishes credibility and creates connection when the story serves the audience's needs. Bragging occurs when the story serves the speaker's ego. The test: does the experience illuminate something useful for the listener, or does it primarily make the speaker look good? Grant teaches: share experiences generously but always frame them for audience relevance.

### Audio 05: What Are Rhythmic Builds?
Rhythmic builds are parallel, repeating structures that create emotional momentum. Three or more statements in parallel form build to a climax point. The rhythm itself creates anticipation — the audience leans in. Rhythmic builds are the primary tool for making a conclusion land with impact. Think: 'We saw the challenge. We developed the solution. We delivered the result.'

### Audio 06: What Role Do Facts Play in Presentations?
Facts are necessary but not sufficient. Audiences don't act on facts — they act on meaning. Facts must be embedded in images and connected to the audience's reality to produce impact. A single vivid statistic that's properly framed is more powerful than a slide full of data. Grant's rule: never present a fact without also presenting what it means.

### Audio 07: What Are the Differences Between Appealing to Your Audience vs. Pleasing Your Audience?
Appealing means giving the audience what serves their genuine interests, even if uncomfortable. Pleasing means giving them what they want to hear. Short-term, pleasing is easier. Long-term, only appealing builds lasting credibility and trust. Leaders who only please are eventually seen as empty and unreliable. Appealing requires courage; pleasing requires only calculation.

### Audio 08: What Are the Differences Between Openness and Intimacy?
Openness is the willingness to share ideas, experiences, and perspectives broadly. Intimacy is the quality of making each listener feel personally addressed. The most powerful communicators achieve both simultaneously — they're transparent and generous with their thinking while making each person feel they're the sole audience. This combination is rare and extremely compelling.

### Audio 09: How Do You Demonstrate Leadership in Your Board Presentation?
Board presentations require a different mindset than peer presentations. Board members want leadership, not reports — they need to see that the presenter has synthesized complexity into clear judgment. Lead with your recommendation, not your data journey. Demonstrate that you've thought further ahead than the current question. Use their time as if it's the most valuable resource in the room, because to them, it is.

### Audio 10: What Are the Steps to Developing Effective Introductions?
The introduction's only job is to establish linkage — connecting the speaker's message to the audience's reality. Grant's formula: start with the audience's world (not yours), identify the gap or challenge they face, promise that what follows will address it. Avoid: opening with your credentials, starting with a joke, or beginning with 'Today I'm going to talk about X.' Begin where the audience already is.


## 6. Application Rules — Translating to Written Content

These rules translate Anett Grant's oral communication principles into written content — blog posts, LinkedIn posts, reports, emails, and strategic documents.

### Rule 1: Start With the Audience's Reality, Not Your Own
**Speaking principle:** Introductions must establish linkage — beginning where the audience already is.  
**Written application:** Open every piece of writing with a sentence or paragraph that reflects the reader's current situation, problem, or question. Don't open with "I want to talk about..." or your credentials. Open with *their* world.

> **Wrong:** "I've spent 24 years in supply chain, and I want to share my perspective on AI adoption..."  
> **Right:** "Most supply chain leaders are drowning in data and starving for decisions. Here's what that gap actually costs you."

---

### Rule 2: Build to a Core, Then Stay There
**Speaking principle:** The Core-Satellite System — one central message that all satellites support.  
**Written application:** Every piece of writing needs a single core claim — one sentence that captures the central argument. Every section, paragraph, and example must connect back to it. If a paragraph doesn't support the core, cut it.

> **Core test:** Can you state your main point in one sentence? If not, you don't have a core yet — you have a topic.

---

### Rule 3: Use Images, Not Just Facts
**Speaking principle:** Facts require images to create meaning and retention.  
**Written application:** Every data point, finding, or claim needs a concrete image alongside it — a specific example, a comparison, a visual metaphor. Never present a statistic without a story or image that makes it real.

> **Wrong:** "AI adoption in supply chain is increasing rapidly."  
> **Right:** "Three years ago, our planning cycle took 12 days. With AI-assisted forecasting, it's now 4 hours — and our error rate dropped by 30%."

---

### Rule 4: Lead With Your Conclusion (Especially for Leadership Audiences)
**Speaking principle:** Upward communication — lead with conclusions, not methodology.  
**Written application:** In any document targeting senior leaders or decision-makers, the recommendation or conclusion goes first. Then the rationale. Never make a busy executive read to the third page to find out what you think they should do.

> **Structure:** Recommendation → Rationale → Evidence → Call to Action

---

### Rule 5: Influence, Don't Just Educate
**Speaking principle:** Influencing changes behavior; educating provides information.  
**Written application:** Before presenting information, establish *why it matters* — what's at stake, what problem it solves, why the reader should care. Then provide the information. Don't reverse this sequence.

> **Educating:** "Here are five things to know about AI in logistics..."  
> **Influencing:** "Your competitors are making decisions in hours that take your team days. Here's the system that changes that — and the five things you need to understand to implement it."

---

### Rule 6: Build Rhythmic Structure (for Emphasis and Memorability)
**Speaking principle:** Rhythmic builds create emotional momentum.  
**Written application:** Use parallel structures for key points, lists, and summaries. The rhythm creates memorability. Three parallel items is the optimal number.

> **Example:** "We reduced cost. We improved speed. We eliminated rework."  
> More powerful than: "We managed to achieve a cost reduction while also improving the speed of the process and we also eliminated rework at the same time."

---

### Rule 7: Strong, Not Hard — Warm, Not Soft
**Speaking principle:** Hard talks at; Strong talks with. Appealing, not just pleasing.  
**Written application:** Write with directness and conviction, but never with condescension or aggression. Give the reader what they need to hear (even if it challenges their assumptions), not just what confirms existing beliefs. Use "you" generously to stay audience-centered.

---

### Rule 8: Close with the Core, Not a Summary
**Speaking principle:** Strong closes return to the core message with renewed emphasis.  
**Written application:** Don't end with "In conclusion, here's everything I said again." End with the core message restated with fresh language and a call to action. The last sentence is what readers remember.

> **Weak close:** "In summary, AI adoption in supply chain offers many benefits..."  
> **Strong close:** "The leaders who move first on this won't just be more efficient — they'll be making decisions their competitors can't even see yet. The question isn't whether to adopt this. It's whether you want to lead or follow."

---

### Rule 9: Reveal Your Thinking (Not Just Your Conclusions)
**Speaking principle:** Leaders who reveal personality and process build faster trust.  
**Written application:** Don't just state conclusions — briefly show the reasoning or the question you wrestled with. This signals intellectual honesty, not uncertainty. Readers trust writers who demonstrate they've actually thought through the hard questions.

---

### Rule 10: Speaking Is Music; Writing Is Film
**Speaking principle:** Speaking = rhythm, dynamics, live performance. Writing = structure, pacing, visual construction.  
**Written application:** Don't write the way you speak (run-on, associative, rambling). Use structure deliberately: short sentences for emphasis, longer ones for elaboration. Use white space, headers, and section breaks to control reading pace. Every structural choice is a directorial decision.

---
