### BLAB vs. BLUF (2026-03-16)
The failure pattern in executive writing is BLAB — Bottom Line At the Bottom. Writers know their conclusion but bury it because they're attached to their reasoning process. Review protocol: explicit "BLUF check" — if the Judgment isn't in the first paragraph, the document is BLAB.

### Story Structure is Cognitive Architecture (2026-03-11/13)
Facts are 22x more memorable inside a story arc than as bare data (Springer). Story frameworks work because they align with *decision-making psychology*, not just emotional engagement. Structure = strategic tool. A pharmaceutical executive secured $47M by changing data structure, not the data. Lead with the wound (problem the audience already feels), not the cure.

### Audience-Centered = Subtraction Discipline (2026-03-12)
Audience-centering is about prioritizing what gets *included at all*, not just adjusting tone. The discipline is cutting content the audience doesn't need — "more will be communicated clearly." Pre-flight gate: "Does my audience need this, or do I just feel better having included it?"

### Clarity-Seeking Behaviors Can Masquerade as Rigor (2026-03-18)
The default leadership response to complexity — gather more data, invite more perspectives — looks responsible but quietly prolongs ambiguity. Withholding a clear recommendation under the guise of "providing options" transfers cognitive burden to the reader. Analytical writing that ends without a clear recommendation is a form of abdication. The test: can the executive make a decision without asking a follow-up question? (Forbes, March 17, 2026)

### Crisis Alignment Is the Real Deliverable — Not the Message (2026-03-23)
S&A Communications + Edelman Trust Barometer (March 2026): the leading cause of failed crisis communication is internal misalignment, not bad messaging. Organizations that manage disruption credibly do so because they built alignment before the crisis — clear decision authority, escalation paths, and shared definition of "good" under stress. The Boeing 737 MAX is the canonical failure: reputational damage compounded not from messaging quality but from visible gaps between what was said and what operationally changed. Key principle: **"Stakeholders measure credibility by what changes, not by what is said."**
Application to LinkedIn: don't position AI as the answer; position AI as the work. The practitioner who describes the hard parts builds more trust than the one who describes only outcomes.

### Supply Chain Boards Now Speak "Total Value" — Not Just Resilience (2026-03-23)

### Readability Is a Trust Signal — The "Quicksand Test" (2026-03-23)
Hurley Write (Aug 2025): readability is not the last editing step; it is a first-principles design decision about the reader. Diagnostic: "Would a CFO who spends 90 seconds on this get the core message and the recommended action?" For LinkedIn, the friction threshold is one confusing sentence that causes a scroll-past.

### Story Structure Frameworks — Six Templates, One Decision Rule (2026-03-23 PM)
Moxie Institute (March 2026): structure is not stylistic — it determines whether narrative can be processed and retained. Decision rule: choose based on objective, not preference. If the reader must act → Problem-Solution or Challenge-Choice-Consequence. If they need belief in change → What Is/What Could Be. If they need context → Before-During-After. Hero's Journey is least useful in executive writing — centers the author, not the reader.
IMfA nominated: YES.

### Primary Reader Strategy — Writing for "Everyone" Is Writing for No One (2026-03-23 PM)
Pryor Learning + Britney Mack (2025): the most common cause of unclear business writing is ambiguous primary audience. Pre-writing gate: name the one person this is most for and the specific belief to shift.
